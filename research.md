# **Before Rice**

<p style="text-align: justify">
As a senior thesis student in the <a href="https://softlivingmatter.com/">Brangwynne group</a> at Princeton, I developed a microfluidic device for mechanically immobilizing active nematodes (C. elegans) during consecutive stages of larval development. Our device enables isolating, feeding, and progressively imaging subcellular features within individual organisms throughout all stages of development, without using a chemical anesthetic. To achieve immobilization, the device takes advantage of hydraulic pressure-driven deformation of a flexible PDMS membrane.</p>

<p style="text-align: justify">
Using this device, we observed age-dependent changes in the diffusivity of two protein condensates within nucleoli (<5 μm) of unique intestinal cells in isolated individuals throughout larval growth. We measured a decrease in the mobility of the nucleolar protein FIB1, which was shown in prior work to undergo aging-related gelation in vitro. The <a href="https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10404-017-1988-2">paper</a> is available in Microfluidics and Nanofluidics and on <a href="https://www.biorxiv.org/content/early/2017/05/19/139915">bioRxiv</a>.</p>

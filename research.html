My work broadly focuses on the nonlinear viscoelasticity of networks of biopolymers like actin and collagen, which mechanically stabilize living cells and tissues. These exhibit rich mechanical responses including strain-stiffening and negative normal stress, and I aim to understand these phenomena using a combination of theory and simple computational models.

Areas of interest

  • Strain stiffening in biopolymer networks

    While biopolymer networks respond softly to very small deformations, they tend to stiffen significantly when subjected to sufficiently large shear or extensile strains. Recent work has shown that, in networks of athermal filaments (i.e. filaments that are sufficiently stiff to justify ignoring thermal fluctuations) like collagen bundles, this stiffening represents a crossover between bending-dominated and stretching-dominated elastic regimes, controlled by an underlying mechanical phase transition (see Sharma, A. et al., Nat Phys 12, 584–587 (2016), Rens, R. et al., J Phys Chem B 120, 5831–5841 (2016), and Jansen, K. A. et al., Biophys J 114, 2665–2678 (2018)).

    In our work, we have explored how this underlying transition affects various mechanical nonlinearities that show up in strained networks, including the development of a normal stress difference in networks under applied simple shear [1] and the shear response of networks subjected to bulk and uniaxial prestress [2]. We also developed a scaling theory for the shear-driven stiffening transition [3] and explored the associated finite size effects [4].

Before Rice

As a senior thesis student in the Brangwynne group at Princeton, I developed a microfluidic device for mechanically immobilizing active nematodes (C. elegans) during consecutive stages of larval development. Our device enables isolating, feeding, and progressively imaging subcellular features within individual organisms throughout all stages of development, without using a chemical anesthetic. To achieve immobilization, the device (detailed in our paper) takes advantage of hydraulic pressure-driven deformation of a flexible PDMS membrane.

Using this device, we observed age-dependent changes in the diffusivity of two protein condensates within nucleoli (<5 μm) of unique intestinal cells in isolated individuals throughout larval growth. We measured a decrease in the mobility of the nucleolar protein FIB1, which was shown in prior work to undergo aging-related gelation in vitro. The full paper is available in Microfluidics and Nanofluidics and on bioRxiv.
